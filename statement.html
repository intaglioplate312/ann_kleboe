Artist's Statment

I feel powerful when I use a pneumatic hammer on limestone, alabaster, or marble. I get a sense of rhythm and movement during the sounds of whacking at the stone. My desire to be unique can be realized in stone.

Have you seen and heard a worker with jackhammer breaking up concrete on sidewalks or streets? This is an intense amount of noise. Now see a mini jack hammer as a pneumatic hammer that gets its energy from an air compressor which fits under my basement stairs.

I protect my ears with earplugs and construction earmuffs too. Still I hear the noise and I move with rhythm which pulls me into a world of power.

A class at Adler Institute helped me understand how my artwork evolves in a process similar to the therapeutic process. First, as a sculptor of stone, I work with the natural flaws in the stone as they occur.

Hacking away at the stone without any conception of what it will be, I create chaos, unless it is already there. Using the inherent flaws and my own errors in the stone as groundwork and as an integral part of the sculpture, I then begin to restore order, bringing out and enhancing the strengths and eliminating the weaknesses. I love depth and smooth travel arises with first with files then abrasive sand papers.

Yes, if you touch them you may want to own one. One time an observer at Old Town Art Fair was touching and moving her hands all over a sculpture, an art dealer Joyce Petter was watching then she asked me if I would show my work in her gallery in Saugatuck, Michigan. She sold a lot of my work from her gallery then called Prajna Gallery. The Art Institute of Chicago also sold my work in their Sales and Rental Gallery.
